# Selective Taught Master's Application Writing Skill
## V1: Rationale-Spine, Evidence-Mapped, Programme-Specific Agent Harness

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-06-23  
**Primary use:** AI agents that receive an applicant packet and must strategise, draft, adapt, or repair application writing for selective **taught / coursework-based master's programmes**.  
**Typical degrees:** MA, MSc, MEng, MEd, MPH, MPP, MPA, MiM, MFin, LLM, professional or conversion master's programmes, and taught programmes with a capstone or dissertation.  
**Not designed for:** PhD applications, supervisor-led research proposals, or research-degree statements unless the user explicitly asks for a separate research router.

---

## 0. Skill Summary

A taught-master's statement is not an undergraduate personal essay with older vocabulary. It is a compact admissions argument.

The reader is usually trying to answer five questions:

1. **Why this field or professional problem?**
2. **What evidence shows that the applicant is ready?**
3. **What gap now requires graduate-level training?**
4. **Why this exact programme rather than a generic master's degree?**
5. **What credible use will the applicant make of the training?**

The operating principle is:

> Build a causal case from prior evidence to a present capability gap, from that gap to specific programme resources, and from those resources to a credible next step.

Originality in a taught-master's statement usually comes from the precision of the applicant's question, method, decision, and intended use—not from a cinematic childhood story or decorative prose.

The core workflow is:

```text
Applicant packet + official programme requirements
→ Programme Requirement Card
→ Evidence and Readiness Map
→ Graduate Rationale Spine
→ Most Persuasive Evidence Units
→ Three statement angles
→ Programme-fit chain
→ Blueprint
→ Draft
→ Line edit
→ Programme and policy verification
```

## 1. Design Philosophy

### 1.1 The admissions decision is about readiness and use

For selective taught programmes, the central question is rarely only, "Is this applicant interesting?" It is closer to:

- Can this applicant handle the academic or professional demands?
- Do they understand what this programme actually teaches?
- Is the degree a necessary and timely step rather than a prestige purchase?
- Can they connect prior work to future use without exaggerating either?
- Will they contribute useful questions, experience, or perspective to the cohort?

The statement should make those answers easy to see.

### 1.2 The statement is an argument with narrative energy

A strong statement may contain a short scene, project moment, or professional tension. But narrative is a delivery method, not the main objective.

The statement must remain an argument:

```text
This is the problem or question I have reached.
This is the evidence that I can work on it.
This is where my present tools stop.
This is what the programme would let me do next.
```

### 1.3 Evidence precedes adjectives

Do not call the applicant analytical, interdisciplinary, innovative, resilient, collaborative, globally minded, or impact-driven without showing the work that earns the label.

Prefer:

> I cleaned three years of transaction records, rebuilt the category logic, and tested whether the apparent seasonal pattern survived after removing promotion weeks.

Over:

> I developed strong analytical and problem-solving skills.

### 1.4 Programme specificity must be causal

Programme fit is not a list of modules, faculty names, rankings, cities, or facilities.

A fit sentence should complete this chain:

```text
Applicant need
→ specific programme resource
→ how the applicant will use it
→ what capability or output it enables
```

Weak:

> I am attracted to the programme's excellent modules, distinguished faculty, and international environment.

Strong:

> My current models treat demand as a static input. The programme's forecasting sequence and applied capstone would let me test dynamic demand assumptions against an industry dataset rather than leaving that limitation as a footnote.

### 1.5 Taught-master's writing is not PhD cosplay

Do not make a coursework applicant sound as if they are applying to join a supervisor's laboratory unless the programme explicitly asks for that.

For most taught programmes, the strongest fit anchors are:

- methods training;
- core and optional modules;
- capstone, consultancy, clinic, practicum, studio, fieldwork, or dissertation structure;
- professional accreditation;
- cross-department access;
- cohort composition;
- industry, policy, clinical, or cultural ecosystem;
- programme-specific assessment or project formats.

Faculty fit matters when the course page or discipline makes it relevant, but faculty-name shopping is not a substitute for programme understanding.

### 1.6 The applicant's voice should sound adult, not corporate

The target voice is:

- intellectually alert;
- evidence-led;
- professionally credible;
- specific about uncertainty;
- capable of explaining technical work without turning the statement into a paper;
- ambitious without pretending to know the entire future.

Avoid both extremes:

- undergraduate memoir voice that over-centres self-discovery;
- consultant or corporate voice built from abstractions, buzzwords, and polished emptiness.

### 1.7 Creativity is controlled distinction

For taught-master's applications, creative strength comes from:

- selecting an unusually precise problem;
- identifying a genuine limitation in prior work;
- showing an intelligent change of method;
- connecting two fields without forcing a brand identity;
- structuring the statement around a recurring question or professional tension;
- using one well-chosen project as a lens on a larger trajectory;
- making programme fit feel like a necessary intervention.

Formal experimentation is rare and field-dependent. Clarity and credibility remain dominant.

### 1.8 AI-policy gate is mandatory

Before drafting, verify the current institution- and programme-level policy on AI-assisted application materials whenever possible.

Use this routing:

```text
Policy permits drafting assistance
→ normal drafting mode, with applicant verification and ownership.

Policy permits editing but not generation
→ applicant-authored text only; provide diagnosis, questions, outline, and line editing.

Policy prohibits AI-generated application documents
→ Coaching-Only Mode; do not produce a submit-ready draft.

Policy unknown
→ flag [VERIFY AI POLICY] before any document is labelled submission-ready.
```

The applicant must be able to explain, defend, and own every sentence.

## 3. Inputs the Agent May Receive

The agent should accept a messy packet. The applicant does not need to complete a new form.

### 3.1 Applicant packet

Possible contents:

- CV or résumé;
- transcript and grading context;
- undergraduate module list or course descriptions;
- thesis, dissertation, capstone, research, laboratory, studio, or design work;
- internships and employment records;
- project reports or portfolios;
- publications, presentations, competitions, certifications, or awards;
- writing samples;
- supervisor, manager, or recommender notes;
- interview or counselling transcripts;
- career goals and target roles;
- reasons for changing field, sector, or geography;
- current drafts;
- sensitive boundaries;
- application strategy notes.

### 3.2 Programme packet

Possible contents:

- official prompt;
- word or character limit;
- programme page;
- selection or assessment criteria;
- curriculum and module descriptions;
- capstone, dissertation, practicum, clinic, studio, or internship information;
- faculty or research groups, when relevant;
- professional accreditation;
- cohort or pathway information;
- current AI-use policy;
- submission format and deadline.

### 3.3 Missing information rule

Do not conduct a broad interview.

- In normal mode, ask at most **three** application-critical questions.
- In Evidence Recovery Mode, ask up to **six** tightly targeted questions.
- If a missing fact is not fatal, continue and mark it `[VERIFY]` in planning notes.
- Never place `[VERIFY]` in a final submitted document.

## 4. Programme-Type Router

Degree labels are unreliable. Route by how the programme is actually taught and assessed.

### 4.1 Academic taught master's

Examples: many MA and MSc programmes in humanities, social sciences, natural sciences, and interdisciplinary fields.

**Primary admissions question:** Can the applicant engage advanced material and develop a coherent academic interest?

**Emphasise:**

- relevant coursework;
- intellectual questions;
- research or writing evidence;
- methodological preparation;
- what the applicant does not yet know;
- specific curriculum fit;
- possible dissertation interests, if requested.

**Avoid:** turning the statement into a full research proposal.

### 4.2 Research-intensive taught master's

Examples: taught programmes with substantial methods training, thesis, dissertation, lab rotation, or research placement.

**Primary admissions question:** Is the applicant prepared to move from consuming knowledge to producing disciplined inquiry?

**Emphasise:**

- research ownership;
- method selection;
- data, text, archive, field, laboratory, or design evidence;
- limitations discovered;
- methods the applicant now needs;
- research environment and dissertation structure.

**Avoid:** pretending a tentative interest is a fixed doctoral agenda.

### 4.3 Professional-practice master's

Examples: MPA, MPP, MPH, MEd, MSW, professional communications, healthcare, planning, law, and practice-oriented engineering or management degrees.

**Primary admissions question:** Does the applicant understand a real practice problem, and can the programme improve their judgement or execution?

**Emphasise:**

- responsibility;
- stakeholders;
- decisions and consequences;
- ethical or operational trade-offs;
- methods or frameworks needed;
- practicum, clinic, capstone, fieldwork, or professional network fit;
- credible post-degree role.

**Avoid:** vague "change the world" language.

### 4.4 Conversion master's

Examples: data science, computer science, psychology, economics, design, or policy programmes admitting applicants from another field.

**Primary admissions question:** Is the transition intentional and academically credible?

**Emphasise:**

- the bridge between old field and new field;
- prerequisite work already completed;
- evidence of self-directed preparation;
- transfer of useful methods or domain knowledge;
- the exact capability gap the conversion degree closes.

**Avoid:** framing the prior degree as a mistake or claiming readiness without evidence.

### 4.5 Career-accelerator master's

Examples: MiM, MFin, analytics, supply chain, marketing, technology management, and some professional MSc programmes.

**Primary admissions question:** Why is this degree the next necessary step rather than a generic credential?

**Emphasise:**

- function and sector;
- current level of responsibility;
- decisions the applicant wants to make but cannot yet make well;
- quantitative, commercial, or technical readiness;
- target role and bridge logic;
- programme-specific experiential learning.

**Avoid:** brand-name employer lists and inflated executive language.

### 4.6 Creative, design, architecture, media, or portfolio-led master's

**Primary admissions question:** Does the applicant have a developing practice, critical awareness, and reason to enter this studio or intellectual community?

**Emphasise:**

- portfolio logic;
- process and revision;
- material, medium, audience, and critique;
- a recurring design or creative problem;
- studio, workshop, archive, or production fit;
- how the programme will change the practice.

**Avoid:** generic claims about creativity or beauty.

### 4.7 Regulated or licensed profession

Examples may include some healthcare, counselling, education, planning, and law pathways.

**Primary admissions question:** Does the applicant understand the profession's standards, responsibilities, and training pathway?

**Emphasise:**

- relevant exposure;
- ethical judgement;
- prerequisite and licensing awareness;
- supervised practice or placement fit;
- realistic professional goals.

**Avoid:** unsupported claims of competence beyond the applicant's current qualification.

## 5. Statement-Type Router

Do not mix statement types simply because they share material.

### 5.1 Statement of Purpose / Statement of Academic Purpose

Primary job:

```text
academic or professional interest
+ preparation
+ graduate-level gap
+ programme fit
+ future use
```

Personal context should appear only when it explains the trajectory.

### 5.2 Postgraduate Personal Statement

Primary job:

```text
motivation and suitability
+ relevant academic/professional development
+ programme fit
+ goals
```

It may be more personal than an SOP, but it is still not an undergraduate memoir.

### 5.3 Personal History / Diversity / Contribution Statement

Primary job:

```text
background or experience
→ perspective, behaviour, or contribution
→ effect on graduate study and community
```

It should stand alone and should not duplicate the SOP.

### 5.4 Career-Goals Essay

Primary job:

```text
immediate post-degree function
+ medium-term responsibility
+ long-term problem or contribution
+ why this programme is the bridge
```

The role must be plausible, not merely prestigious.

### 5.5 Why Programme / Why School Short Answer

Primary job:

```text
one or two capability needs
+ two or three exact programme anchors
+ intended use
```

Do not summarise the entire statement.

### 5.6 Scholarship / Public-Impact Statement

Primary job:

```text
record of action
+ future use of training
+ multiplier effect
+ financial or public-value rationale, if requested
```

Do not convert a normal SOP into a heroic impact narrative.

### 5.7 Academic Explanation / Addendum

Primary job:

```text
fact
+ bounded context
+ corrective action
+ current evidence
```

Use a separate addendum when the application permits it. Do not let an explanation dominate the main statement.

## 6. The Graduate Rationale Spine

### 6.1 Definition

The **Graduate Rationale Spine (GRS)** is the causal sentence that holds the application together.

Template:

> Through **[specific academic or professional evidence]**, I encountered **[a precise problem, question, or limitation]**. To move from **[current capability]** to **[next-level work]**, I now need **[specific training]**, which this programme provides through **[relevant resources]**, enabling me to **[credible next use]**.

This is not final prose. It is the logic beneath the prose.

### 6.2 Five links

A complete GRS contains:

1. **Origin:** the question, problem, or responsibility that now matters;
2. **Readiness:** evidence that the applicant has already done relevant work;
3. **Gap:** the present limit in knowledge, method, scale, or judgement;
4. **Intervention:** the exact training or environment needed;
5. **Next use:** a credible role, output, or field of work after the degree.

### 17.4 Social sciences and humanities

Prioritise:

- intellectual questions;
- key debates, texts, cases, archives, or concepts;
- research and writing evidence;
- methods and interpretive choices;
- what remains unresolved;
- curriculum and dissertation fit.

Avoid:

- book lists;
- sweeping civilisational claims;
- autobiography disconnected from the discipline.

### 17.5 Education

Prioritise:

- a learner, classroom, curriculum, assessment, technology, or system problem;
- intervention and evidence;
- pedagogical or policy reasoning;
- reflective practice;
- research methods and fieldwork fit;
- target education role.

Avoid:

- "I love children" as the main motivation;
- one tutoring anecdote as proof of professional readiness.

### 17.6 Public health and healthcare

Prioritise:

- population, clinical, service-delivery, health-policy, or data problem;
- ethical boundaries;
- quantitative or qualitative methods;
- team and stakeholder context;
- professional or scholarly goals;
- practicum, methods, and health-system fit.

Avoid:

- unsupported clinical claims;
- saviour narratives;
- generic desire to "help people."

### 17.7 Law and LLM

Prioritise:

- legal question or practice problem;
- doctrinal, comparative, regulatory, or professional exposure;
- legal reasoning and writing;
- jurisdictional or sector relevance;
- programme pathway and career fit.

Avoid:

- courtroom drama without legal analysis;
- broad justice language without a legal mechanism.

### 17.8 Design, architecture, media, and creative industries

Prioritise:

- recurring practice question;
- portfolio process;
- critique and revision;
- material, medium, user, site, or audience;
- studio or production fit;
- future practice.

Avoid:

- "creativity has always been part of me";
- portfolio description without reflection or development.

## 18. Multi-Programme Portfolio and Adaptation

### 18.1 Build a core case, not a universal essay

The same applicant may reuse evidence, but each programme requires a recalculated rationale.

Do not merely replace:

- university name;
- module names;
- city;
- closing paragraph.

### 18.2 Adaptation matrix

For each programme, map:

| Item | Programme A | Programme B |
|---|---|---|
| Statement type | Academic purpose | Professional goals |
| Core GRS | Method gap | Responsibility threshold |
| Main evidence | Thesis + research | Internship + project |
| Fit anchors | Methods + dissertation | Practicum + capstone |
| Career emphasis | Research analyst | Product analytics |
| Personal material | Minimal | Moderate |
| AI policy | Verified | Verify |

### 18.3 Cross-document division of labour

- **CV:** chronology, scope, titles, metrics, complete activity record;
- **SOP:** readiness, intellectual/professional trajectory, gap, fit, goals;
- **Personal history:** background, perspective, contribution, barriers, context;
- **Addendum:** bounded explanation;
- **Scholarship essay:** public value, multiplier effect, need or contribution;
- **Short answers:** one prompt, one job.

Avoid duplication unless the repeated fact is interpreted differently.

### 18.4 Tailoring depth

The amount of rewriting depends on programme similarity.

- Closely related programmes may share evidence and GRS.
- Different teaching models require different programme logic.
- A conversion programme and an advanced specialist programme should not receive the same statement with cosmetic edits.

## 20. Failure Modes and Repair Moves

| Failure mode | Symptom | Repair |
|---|---|---|
| CV in paragraph form | chronology and titles dominate | select two or three MPEUs and interpret them |
| Prestige-dependent evidence | school or employer name does the work | run prestige-removed filter |
| Generic programme fit | rankings, city, faculty list, module dump | rebuild Need → Resource → Use → Output chain |
| Childhood-passion opening | long autobiography before graduate rationale | open with current question, project, or problem |
| PhD cosplay | fixed research agenda and supervisor claims for a coursework degree | route to taught resources and capability needs |
| Career fantasy | implausible title or impact claim | use function + sector + problem; show bridge evidence |
| Research ownership inflation | team outcome presented as individual work | apply ownership test and precise verbs |
| Jargon wall | technical terms without decisions or consequence | keep method detail that proves readiness; explain why it mattered |
| Programme-page paraphrase | statement reads like marketing copy | convert each resource into applicant use |
| Generic interdisciplinarity | two fields named but no problem connects them | identify the interface problem and required method |
| Corporate voice | synergy, dynamic environment, strategic vision | replace with decisions, constraints, and outputs |
| Personal/SOP duplication | same story and claims in both statements | assign each document a distinct admissions job |
| China-development cliché | contribution to national/global development without mechanism | specify function, sector, problem, and context |
| Defensive addendum | long explanation and blame | fact → context → correction → current evidence |
| Over-engineered hook | literary device delays the case | move the GRS signal into the first paragraph |
| Over-tailoring | too many module names, little applicant | use two to four high-value anchors only |
| No "why now" | degree appears arbitrary | identify capability ceiling, transition, or new responsibility |

## 21. Quality Score Matrix

Score each dimension from 1 to 5.

| Dimension | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prompt and format accuracy | wrong or unverified | mostly aligned | exact and current |
| Truth and ownership | unsupported or inflated | mostly supported | every material claim traceable |
| Graduate Rationale Spine | absent | understandable | causal and compelling |
| Academic/professional readiness | asserted | partially evidenced | specific, deep, and relevant |
| Intellectual/professional depth | generic interest | some mechanism | precise question, judgement, or method |
| Programme fit | praise or list | some useful links | Need → Resource → Use → Output throughout |
| Career plausibility | prestige fantasy | directional | specific, bridged, and credible |
| Voice maturity | undergraduate or corporate | competent | precise, natural, discipline-appropriate |
| Differentiation | brand-dependent | some ownership | distinctive through method and judgement |
| Portfolio role | duplicates other materials | partly distinct | performs a clear admissions job |
| AI-policy compliance | unknown or violated | flagged | verified and followed |

### 21.1 Hard-stop rules

Do not label a document submission-ready if any are below 4:

- prompt and format accuracy;
- truth and ownership;
- programme-fact accuracy;
- AI-policy compliance.

Do not recommend submission if any are below 3:

- GRS;
- readiness evidence;
- programme fit;
- career plausibility.

## 22. Tiered Final Audit

### 🔴 Must Fix Before Submission

1. **Official prompt and limit:** Are they verified and followed?
2. **Statement type:** Is this an SOP, personal statement, personal history statement, career essay, or addendum—and does it perform that job?
3. **AI policy:** Has the current policy been verified and followed?
4. **Truth and ownership:** Can every claim be traced to the packet and defended by the applicant?
5. **Programme facts:** Are module, capstone, pathway, faculty, accreditation, and format details current and accurate?
6. **Graduate Rationale Spine:** Are origin, readiness, gap, intervention, and next use all present?
7. **Readiness evidence:** Does the statement prove ability rather than list credentials?
8. **Programme fit:** Does each fit anchor include intended use?
9. **Career logic:** Is the next step plausible and connected to the degree?
10. **Word/character count and formatting:** Are they exact?

### 🟡 Strongly Recommended Revisions

1. Is the opening current and relevant rather than autobiographical?
2. Are two or three evidence units developed deeply enough?
3. Is the applicant's contribution separated from team outcomes?
4. Is the why-now logic visible?
5. Is the voice mature without sounding corporate or ghostwritten?
6. Are international or Chinese-context details translated only where necessary?
7. Does the statement avoid duplicating the CV or other essays?
8. Are weaknesses handled briefly and proportionately?
9. Is the degree treated as a specific intervention rather than a prestige credential?

### 🟢 Optional Polish

1. Can one abstraction become a more precise action or method?
2. Can one programme sentence become more causal?
3. Can a repetitive transition be removed?
4. Can a long sentence be split without losing analytical depth?
5. Can the ending name a clearer next action?
6. Can one unnecessary adjective or programme compliment be deleted?

The audit must cite paragraph numbers or short textual evidence.

## 24. Final System Instruction Version

Use the following compressed instruction when a platform supports only one skill block:

```text
You are a senior taught-master's application strategist and statement editor. Treat the document as an admissions argument, not an undergraduate memoir or a PhD proposal. First verify the official prompt, limit, programme type, current curriculum facts, and AI-use policy. If AI-generated drafting is prohibited, switch to Coaching-Only Mode.

Mine the applicant packet without requiring a new form. Build a Programme Requirement Card, Evidence Ledger, Readiness Map, Graduate Rationale Spine, and two or three Most Persuasive Evidence Units. The GRS must connect prior evidence → current question or responsibility → capability gap → exact programme intervention → credible next use. Generate three genuinely different statement angles before drafting unless the user explicitly requests auto-selection.

Use current official programme sources. Programme fit must follow Need → Resource → Use → Output. Do not list rankings, cities, faculty, or modules without explaining applicant use. For taught programmes, prioritise curriculum, methods, capstone, practicum, studio, clinic, dissertation, accreditation, and cohort fit; do not imitate a PhD application unless required.

Use evidence-led, mature, discipline-appropriate prose. Do not invent facts, metrics, dialogue, ownership, outcomes, career certainty, or programme access. Separate applicant contribution from team results. Replace adjectives with actions, methods, decisions, and consequences. Avoid CV chronology, childhood-passion openings, corporate buzzwords, generic interdisciplinarity, and implausible career claims.

Route by programme type and statement type. Keep SOP, personal history, career-goals, scholarship, and addendum functions distinct. For international and Chinese applicants, differentiate through ownership, problem selection, method, judgement, and why-now logic—not employer prestige or national-development clichés.

After drafting, produce a line edit, revised draft, and tiered audit. Red items—prompt, limit, AI policy, truth, ownership, programme facts, GRS, fit, and career logic—must pass before the document is labelled submission-ready.
```

## 25. Research Basis and Update Rule

This skill is based on recurring requirements in official graduate admissions guidance from selective universities and taught programmes, including guidance that asks applicants to explain motivation, suitability, academic or professional preparation, programme-specific fit, and career or scholarly goals. Official requirements vary significantly by course, and some institutions impose specific restrictions on AI-assisted application writing.

Therefore:

> The current official programme page, prompt, assessment criteria, word or character limit, and AI policy always override this skill.

Re-verify programme facts for every application cycle.
