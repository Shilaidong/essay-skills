# Selective Taught Master's Application Writing Skill
## V1: Rationale-Spine, Evidence-Mapped, Programme-Specific Agent Harness

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-06-23  
**Primary use:** AI agents that receive an applicant packet and must strategise, draft, adapt, or repair application writing for selective **taught / coursework-based master's programmes**.  
**Typical degrees:** MA, MSc, MEng, MEd, MPH, MPP, MPA, MiM, MFin, LLM, professional or conversion master's programmes, and taught programmes with a capstone or dissertation.  
**Not designed for:** PhD applications, supervisor-led research proposals, or research-degree statements unless the user explicitly asks for a separate research router.

---

## 0. Skill Summary

A taught-master's statement is not an undergraduate personal essay with older vocabulary. It is a compact admissions argument.

The reader is usually trying to answer five questions:

1. **Why this field or professional problem?**
2. **What evidence shows that the applicant is ready?**
3. **What gap now requires graduate-level training?**
4. **Why this exact programme rather than a generic master's degree?**
5. **What credible use will the applicant make of the training?**

The operating principle is:

> Build a causal case from prior evidence to a present capability gap, from that gap to specific programme resources, and from those resources to a credible next step.

Originality in a taught-master's statement usually comes from the precision of the applicant's question, method, decision, and intended use—not from a cinematic childhood story or decorative prose.

The core workflow is:

```text
Applicant packet + official programme requirements
→ Programme Requirement Card
→ Evidence and Readiness Map
→ Graduate Rationale Spine
→ Most Persuasive Evidence Units
→ Three statement angles
→ Programme-fit chain
→ Blueprint
→ Draft
→ Line edit
→ Programme and policy verification
```

## 1. Design Philosophy

### 1.1 The admissions decision is about readiness and use

For selective taught programmes, the central question is rarely only, “Is this applicant interesting?” It is closer to:

- Can this applicant handle the academic or professional demands?
- Do they understand what this programme actually teaches?
- Is the degree a necessary and timely step rather than a prestige purchase?
- Can they connect prior work to future use without exaggerating either?
- Will they contribute useful questions, experience, or perspective to the cohort?

The statement should make those answers easy to see.

### 1.2 The statement is an argument with narrative energy

A strong statement may contain a short scene, project moment, or professional tension. But narrative is a delivery method, not the main objective.

The statement must remain an argument:

```text
This is the problem or question I have reached.
This is the evidence that I can work on it.
This is where my present tools stop.
This is what the programme would let me do next.
```

### 1.3 Evidence precedes adjectives

Do not call the applicant analytical, interdisciplinary, innovative, resilient, collaborative, globally minded, or impact-driven without showing the work that earns the label.

Prefer:

> I cleaned three years of transaction records, rebuilt the category logic, and tested whether the apparent seasonal pattern survived after removing promotion weeks.

Over:

> I developed strong analytical and problem-solving skills.

### 1.4 Programme specificity must be causal

Programme fit is not a list of modules, faculty names, rankings, cities, or facilities.

A fit sentence should complete this chain:

```text
Applicant need
→ specific programme resource
→ how the applicant will use it
→ what capability or output it enables
```

Weak:

> I am attracted to the programme's excellent modules, distinguished faculty, and international environment.

Strong:

> My current models treat demand as a static input. The programme's forecasting sequence and applied capstone would let me test dynamic demand assumptions against an industry dataset rather than leaving that limitation as a footnote.

### 1.5 Taught-master's writing is not PhD cosplay

Do not make a coursework applicant sound as if they are applying to join a supervisor's laboratory unless the programme explicitly asks for that.

For most taught programmes, the strongest fit anchors are:

- methods training;
- core and optional modules;
- capstone, consultancy, clinic, practicum, studio, fieldwork, or dissertation structure;
- professional accreditation;
- cross-department access;
- cohort composition;
- industry, policy, clinical, or cultural ecosystem;
- programme-specific assessment or project formats.

Faculty fit matters when the course page or discipline makes it relevant, but faculty-name shopping is not a substitute for programme understanding.

### 1.6 The applicant's voice should sound adult, not corporate

The target voice is:

- intellectually alert;
- evidence-led;
- professionally credible;
- specific about uncertainty;
- capable of explaining technical work without turning the statement into a paper;
- ambitious without pretending to know the entire future.

Avoid both extremes:

- undergraduate memoir voice that over-centres self-discovery;
- consultant or corporate voice built from abstractions, buzzwords, and polished emptiness.

### 1.7 Creativity is controlled distinction

For taught-master's applications, creative strength comes from:

- selecting an unusually precise problem;
- identifying a genuine limitation in prior work;
- showing an intelligent change of method;
- connecting two fields without forcing a brand identity;
- structuring the statement around a recurring question or professional tension;
- using one well-chosen project as a lens on a larger trajectory;
- making programme fit feel like a necessary intervention.

Formal experimentation is rare and field-dependent. Clarity and credibility remain dominant.

### 1.8 AI-policy gate is mandatory

Before drafting, verify the current institution- and programme-level policy on AI-assisted application materials whenever possible.

Use this routing:

```text
Policy permits drafting assistance
→ normal drafting mode, with applicant verification and ownership.

Policy permits editing but not generation
→ applicant-authored text only; provide diagnosis, questions, outline, and line editing.

Policy prohibits AI-generated application documents
→ Coaching-Only Mode; do not produce a submit-ready draft.

Policy unknown
→ flag [VERIFY AI POLICY] before any document is labelled submission-ready.
```

The applicant must be able to explain, defend, and own every sentence.

## 3. Inputs the Agent May Receive

The agent should accept a messy packet. The applicant does not need to complete a new form.

### 3.1 Applicant packet

Possible contents:

- CV or résumé;
- transcript and grading context;
- undergraduate module list or course descriptions;
- thesis, dissertation, capstone, research, laboratory, studio, or design work;
- internships and employment records;
- project reports or portfolios;
- publications, presentations, competitions, certifications, or awards;
- writing samples;
- supervisor, manager, or recommender notes;
- interview or counselling transcripts;
- career goals and target roles;
- reasons for changing field, sector, or geography;
- current drafts;
- sensitive boundaries;
- application strategy notes.

### 3.2 Programme packet

Possible contents:

- official prompt;
- word or character limit;
- programme page;
- selection or assessment criteria;
- curriculum and module descriptions;
- capstone, dissertation, practicum, clinic, studio, or internship information;
- faculty or research groups, when relevant;
- professional accreditation;
- cohort or pathway information;
- current AI-use policy;
- submission format and deadline.

### 3.3 Missing information rule

Do not conduct a broad interview.

- In normal mode, ask at most **three** application-critical questions.
- In Evidence Recovery Mode, ask up to **six** tightly targeted questions.
- If a missing fact is not fatal, continue and mark it `[VERIFY]` in planning notes.
- Never place `[VERIFY]` in a final submitted document.

## 4. Programme-Type Router

Degree labels are unreliable. Route by how the programme is actually taught and assessed.

### 4.1 Academic taught master's

Examples: many MA and MSc programmes in humanities, social sciences, natural sciences, and interdisciplinary fields.

**Primary admissions question:** Can the applicant engage advanced material and develop a coherent academic interest?

**Emphasise:**

- relevant coursework;
- intellectual questions;
- research or writing evidence;
- methodological preparation;
- what the applicant does not yet know;
- specific curriculum fit;
- possible dissertation interests, if requested.

**Avoid:** turning the statement into a full research proposal.

### 4.2 Research-intensive taught master's

Examples: taught programmes with substantial methods training, thesis, dissertation, lab rotation, or research placement.

**Primary admissions question:** Is the applicant prepared to move from consuming knowledge to producing disciplined inquiry?

**Emphasise:**

- research ownership;
- method selection;
- data, text, archive, field, laboratory, or design evidence;
- limitations discovered;
- methods the applicant now needs;
- research environment and dissertation structure.

**Avoid:** pretending a tentative interest is a fixed doctoral agenda.

### 4.3 Professional-practice master's

Examples: MPA, MPP, MPH, MEd, MSW, professional communications, healthcare, planning, law, and practice-oriented engineering or management degrees.

**Primary admissions question:** Does the applicant understand a real practice problem, and can the programme improve their judgement or execution?

**Emphasise:**

- responsibility;
- stakeholders;
- decisions and consequences;
- ethical or operational trade-offs;
- methods or frameworks needed;
- practicum, clinic, capstone, fieldwork, or professional network fit;
- credible post-degree role.

**Avoid:** vague “change the world” language.

### 4.4 Conversion master's

Examples: data science, computer science, psychology, economics, design, or policy programmes admitting applicants from another field.

**Primary admissions question:** Is the transition intentional and academically credible?

**Emphasise:**

- the bridge between old field and new field;
- prerequisite work already completed;
- evidence of self-directed preparation;
- transfer of useful methods or domain knowledge;
- the exact capability gap the conversion degree closes.

**Avoid:** framing the prior degree as a mistake or claiming readiness without evidence.

### 4.5 Career-accelerator master's

Examples: MiM, MFin, analytics, supply chain, marketing, technology management, and some professional MSc programmes.

**Primary admissions question:** Why is this degree the next necessary step rather than a generic credential?

**Emphasise:**

- function and sector;
- current level of responsibility;
- decisions the applicant wants to make but cannot yet make well;
- quantitative, commercial, or technical readiness;
- target role and bridge logic;
- programme-specific experiential learning.

**Avoid:** brand-name employer lists and inflated executive language.

### 4.6 Creative, design, architecture, media, or portfolio-led master's

**Primary admissions question:** Does the applicant have a developing practice, critical awareness, and reason to enter this studio or intellectual community?

**Emphasise:**

- portfolio logic;
- process and revision;
- material, medium, audience, and critique;
- a recurring design or creative problem;
- studio, workshop, archive, or production fit;
- how the programme will change the practice.

**Avoid:** generic claims about creativity or beauty.

### 4.7 Regulated or licensed profession

Examples may include some healthcare, counselling, education, planning, and law pathways.

**Primary admissions question:** Does the applicant understand the profession's standards, responsibilities, and training pathway?

**Emphasise:**

- relevant exposure;
- ethical judgement;
- prerequisite and licensing awareness;
- supervised practice or placement fit;
- realistic professional goals.

**Avoid:** unsupported claims of competence beyond the applicant's current qualification.

## 5. Statement-Type Router

Do not mix statement types simply because they share material.

### 5.1 Statement of Purpose / Statement of Academic Purpose

Primary job:

```text
academic or professional interest
+ preparation
+ graduate-level gap
+ programme fit
+ future use
```

Personal context should appear only when it explains the trajectory.

### 5.2 Postgraduate Personal Statement

Primary job:

```text
motivation and suitability
+ relevant academic/professional development
+ programme fit
+ goals
```

It may be more personal than an SOP, but it is still not an undergraduate memoir.

### 5.3 Personal History / Diversity / Contribution Statement

Primary job:

```text
background or experience
→ perspective, behaviour, or contribution
→ effect on graduate study and community
```

It should stand alone and should not duplicate the SOP.

### 5.4 Career-Goals Essay

Primary job:

```text
immediate post-degree function
+ medium-term responsibility
+ long-term problem or contribution
+ why this programme is the bridge
```

The role must be plausible, not merely prestigious.

### 5.5 Why Programme / Why School Short Answer

Primary job:

```text
one or two capability needs
+ two or three exact programme anchors
+ intended use
```

Do not summarise the entire statement.

### 5.6 Scholarship / Public-Impact Statement

Primary job:

```text
record of action
+ future use of training
+ multiplier effect
+ financial or public-value rationale, if requested
```

Do not convert a normal SOP into a heroic impact narrative.

### 5.7 Academic Explanation / Addendum

Primary job:

```text
fact
+ bounded context
+ corrective action
+ current evidence
```

Use a separate addendum when the application permits it. Do not let an explanation dominate the main statement.

## 6. The Graduate Rationale Spine

### 6.1 Definition

The **Graduate Rationale Spine (GRS)** is the causal sentence that holds the application together.

Template:

> Through **[specific academic or professional evidence]**, I encountered **[a precise problem, question, or limitation]**. To move from **[current capability]** to **[next-level work]**, I now need **[specific training]**, which this programme provides through **[relevant resources]**, enabling me to **[credible next use]**.

This is not final prose. It is the logic beneath the prose.

### 6.2 Five links

A complete GRS contains:

1. **Origin:** the question, problem, or responsibility that now matters;
2. **Readiness:** evidence that the applicant has already done relevant work;
3. **Gap:** the present limit in knowledge, method, scale, or judgement;
4. **Intervention:** the exact training or environment needed;
5. **Next use:** a credible role, output, or field of work after the degree.

### 6.3 Mandatory tests

Ask:

- **Why now?** What changed, accumulated, or became impossible to ignore?
- **Why a taught master's?** Why is structured graduate training the right intervention?
- **Why this programme?** Which resources directly address the identified gap?
- **Why this applicant?** What evidence shows readiness and ownership?
- **What next?** What will the applicant be able to do, decide, build, analyse, or contribute after the programme?

If any answer is generic, the spine is incomplete.

### 6.4 Spine quality levels

**Weak:**

> I have always been interested in finance and want to study at a prestigious university to achieve my career goals.

**Usable:**

> My internship exposed me to valuation work, and I now want stronger finance training before entering investment banking.

**Strong:**

> Rebuilding a valuation model after a sensitivity assumption failed showed me that I could execute the mechanics but not yet judge how industry structure should alter the model. I now need deeper training in corporate finance and applied econometrics to move from model completion to investment judgement.

## 7. Most Persuasive Evidence Units

### 7.1 Definition

A **Most Persuasive Evidence Unit (MPEU)** is a project, responsibility, paper, decision, or sustained experience that proves readiness and advances the GRS.

Most statements need two or three strong MPEUs, not a complete chronology.

### 7.2 Evidence-unit anatomy

For each MPEU, identify:

```text
Context
→ applicant's actual responsibility
→ method or tool used
→ decision, difficulty, or trade-off
→ output or consequence
→ what the applicant can now do
→ what limitation remained
```

### 7.3 Ownership test

For team-based work, answer:

- What did the applicant personally decide, build, analyse, write, test, negotiate, or revise?
- What was assigned, and what was self-initiated?
- Which outcome belongs to the team, and which contribution belongs to the applicant?
- Could the applicant explain the method in an interview?

Do not convert team exposure into individual ownership.

### 7.4 Depth test

A strong evidence unit contains at least three of the following:

- a specific question;
- a named method, framework, material, dataset, text, tool, or process;
- a decision or trade-off;
- a limitation;
- an output;
- a changed method;
- a clear connection to the graduate gap.

### 7.5 Prestige-removed filter

Ask:

> Would this evidence still persuade if the employer, university, award, or client name were removed?

If not, the paragraph depends on institutional prestige rather than applicant work.

## 8. Evidence Ledger and Readiness Map

Before drafting, build an internal evidence ledger.

```markdown
## Evidence Ledger

### Academic Foundation
- Relevant modules, grades, theories, texts, or disciplinary training

### Methods and Tools
- Quantitative, qualitative, technical, laboratory, design, legal, policy, archival, or professional methods

### Projects and Research
- Question, role, method, output, limitation

### Professional Responsibility
- Stakeholders, decisions, constraints, consequences

### Intellectual Questions
- Repeated problems, unresolved questions, field tensions

### Career Evidence
- Steps already taken toward the intended function or sector

### Communication and Collaboration
- Evidence relevant to seminars, cohorts, client work, public communication, or team practice

### Gaps and Risks
- Prerequisite gaps, grade anomalies, career switch, thin evidence, unverifiable claims

### Programme Needs
- Capabilities the applicant must gain

### Verification Flags
- Facts, programme details, dates, metrics, ownership claims, policy status
```

### 8.1 Readiness matrix

Map requirements to evidence:

| Programme demand | Applicant evidence | Strength | Missing proof | Use in statement? |
|---|---|---:|---|---|
| Quantitative readiness | Econometrics course + forecasting project | 4/5 | Clarify software and ownership | Yes |
| Academic writing | Thesis / research paper | 3/5 | Need result or feedback | Maybe |
| Professional context | Two internships | 2/5 | Current bullets are brand-led | Rebuild |

### 8.2 Evidence Recovery Mode trigger

Trigger recovery if two or more are true:

1. The packet is mostly institution names and role titles.
2. No project includes a clear applicant-owned action.
3. The career goal has no evidence of prior exploration.
4. The applicant cannot explain why a taught master's is needed now.
5. Programme fit is based only on rankings, location, or reputation.
6. A conversion applicant has no prerequisite or bridge evidence.
7. The statement prompt requests academic preparation, but the packet contains no usable course, paper, project, or method detail.

Ask up to six targeted questions, such as:

- What was the hardest decision you personally made in this project?
- Which method or tool did you choose, and what alternative did you reject?
- What did the output fail to explain or solve?
- What task in your intended role can you not yet perform reliably?
- Which programme component addresses that gap, and how would you use it?
- What evidence shows that your career transition has already begun?

## 9. Graduate-Appropriate Angle Engines

Generate angles only after the GRS and evidence units are visible.

### 9.1 Problem-of-Practice Engine

Start with a concrete professional or field problem whose consequences the applicant has seen.

Best for:

- policy;
- public health;
- education;
- engineering;
- management;
- planning;
- social work;
- law;
- professional practice.

Structure:

```text
problem encountered
→ current response
→ limit of current tools
→ graduate training needed
→ next use
```

### 9.2 Intellectual Pivot Engine

Build around the moment when an existing explanation became insufficient.

Best for:

- humanities;
- social sciences;
- academic MSc or MA;
- interdisciplinary transitions.

The pivot should be conceptual or methodological, not merely “I became interested.”

### 9.3 Method-Gap Engine

Build around a project that reached the edge of the applicant's methods.

Examples:

- model could describe but not predict;
- interview data revealed a mechanism the survey design missed;
- laboratory protocol isolated a variable but not its field interaction;
- legal analysis identified a rule but not implementation effects;
- design prototype solved usability but not adoption.

This is often the strongest taught-master's angle.

### 9.4 Responsibility-Threshold Engine

Build around a shift from assisting or executing to needing judgement at a higher level.

Examples:

- from producing reports to choosing what evidence matters;
- from implementing a campaign to allocating resources;
- from writing code to designing a reliable system;
- from teaching lessons to evaluating intervention effectiveness.

### 9.5 Cross-Domain Bridge Engine

Use when the applicant's value lies at an interface:

- law + technology;
- biology + policy;
- design + computation;
- finance + climate;
- language + education;
- engineering + public systems.

The bridge must solve a real problem. Do not create an “interdisciplinary” identity for decoration.

### 9.6 Data Anomaly / Unexpected Result Engine

Start from an observation that challenged the applicant's assumption.

This works when the applicant can explain:

- what was expected;
- what appeared instead;
- how they checked it;
- what new question followed;
- why graduate study is needed.

### 9.7 System-versus-Implementation Engine

Use when the applicant knows the theory or policy but has seen implementation fail.

The essay should identify the mechanism of failure, not only complain about the gap between theory and practice.

### 9.8 Career-Inflection Engine

Use for experienced applicants whose current work has created a clear ceiling.

Structure:

```text
current responsibility
→ decision now beyond present training
→ evidence of readiness
→ degree as targeted intervention
→ next level of responsibility
```

### 9.9 Translation / Interface Engine

Use when the applicant repeatedly translates between:

- technical and non-technical teams;
- research and policy;
- client and engineer;
- clinical and community settings;
- local and international systems;
- theory and implementation.

The angle must show what is lost in translation and how the applicant wants to improve the interface.

### 9.10 Constraint-to-Capability Engine

Use when a resource, institutional, regulatory, data, or organisational constraint forced a better method.

Do not turn constraint into a generic resilience story. The point is the judgement developed under constraint.

## 10. Programme-Fit Research Protocol

### 10.1 Official-source rule

Use current official programme and department sources for:

- prompt and word/character limit;
- assessment criteria;
- programme structure;
- modules and pathways;
- capstone, practicum, clinic, studio, fieldwork, dissertation, or internship;
- faculty, if relevant;
- professional accreditation;
- AI policy;
- submission format.

If official information is unavailable, mark the item `[VERIFY OFFICIAL]`.

Do not rely on ranking pages, generic admissions blogs, or outdated module lists for final programme facts.

### 10.2 Programme Requirement Card

Build this before drafting:

```markdown
## Programme Requirement Card
- Institution:
- Programme:
- Degree type / actual teaching model:
- Statement type:
- Official prompt:
- Word or character limit:
- Assessed for:
- Required topics:
- Programme-specific resources:
- Taught / capstone / dissertation balance:
- AI policy status:
- Official information last verified:
```

### 10.3 Fit-chain formula

Every programme-fit anchor should satisfy:

```text
Need → Resource → Use → Output
```

Example:

```text
Need: causal inference for observational health data
Resource: required advanced epidemiology sequence
Use: test whether the association found in current fieldwork survives confounding controls
Output: stronger preparation for evaluation work in a public-health agency
```

### 10.4 Fit-anchor hierarchy

Usually strongest:

1. assessment format or experiential component directly tied to the applicant's gap;
2. methods sequence or pathway;
3. capstone, dissertation, clinic, studio, consultancy, or placement;
4. cross-department resource;
5. faculty or research group, when clearly relevant;
6. cohort or regional ecosystem, when used concretely;
7. ranking, prestige, city, and generic diversity claims—usually weak.

### 10.5 Faculty-name rule

Name a faculty member only when at least two are true:

- the programme page makes faculty research central;
- the applicant has read or used the person's work;
- the intellectual connection is specific;
- the programme allows taught students meaningful access;
- the connection advances the GRS.

Do not imply supervision or access that the programme does not promise.

### 10.6 Fit density test

For each school-specific sentence, ask:

> Does this sentence explain how the applicant will use the programme, or does it only praise the programme?

Delete praise-only sentences.

## 11. Voice Construction for Graduate Applications

### 11.1 Voice is a pattern of judgement

Graduate voice is not advanced vocabulary. It is how the applicant handles evidence, uncertainty, responsibility, and consequence.

### 11.2 Voice axes

Construct the voice along these axes:

1. **Epistemic stance:** exploratory, hypothesis-driven, interpretive, operational, critical;
2. **Analytical granularity:** broad systems, mid-level mechanisms, technical detail;
3. **Professional responsibility:** observer, contributor, owner, manager, practitioner;
4. **Emotional temperature:** restrained, candid, urgent, reflective, dry;
5. **Technical density:** low, moderate, high but readable;
6. **Ambition calibration:** cautious, directional, assertive but plausible;
7. **Sentence motion:** linear, cumulative, contrast-led, question-led, process-led.

### 11.3 Evidence-to-voice mapping

| Packet evidence | Possible voice implication |
|---|---|
| Repeatedly checks assumptions and error sources | precise, self-correcting, method-led |
| Works across technical and client teams | translator, consequence-aware |
| Builds systems or workflows | operational, structured, concrete |
| Engages texts and competing interpretations | analytical, qualified, concept-led |
| Holds responsibility under uncertainty | calm, decision-centred |
| Changes fields through self-study | candid, bridge-building, disciplined |

### 11.4 Voice Bootstrap Protocol

If the packet contains little natural voice, generate three 90–120-word test passages.

They must differ on at least **three** voice axes.

Suggested architectures:

- **A — Scholar-Analyst:** question-led, conceptually precise, restrained;
- **B — Practitioner-Builder:** decision-led, consequence-aware, operational;
- **C — Translator-Strategist:** cross-domain, audience-aware, future-oriented.

Do not merely change synonyms. Identify which evidence supports each architecture, then select one.

### 11.5 Language calibration

Do not over-polish an international applicant into native editorial prose that they could not sustain in interview or writing samples.

Keep:

- accurate disciplinary language;
- natural sentence variation;
- occasional directness;
- the applicant's real level of abstraction.

Remove:

- grammatical errors that obstruct meaning;
- translated clichés;
- corporate filler;
- pseudo-academic complexity.

## 12. China-Context and High-Homogeneity Differentiation

### 12.1 The differentiation problem

Many applicants present similar surface materials:

- high GPA;
- several internships;
- competition awards;
- research assistant titles;
- student-society leadership;
- overseas exchange;
- brand-name employers;
- broad claims about international perspective.

Do not search for a “unique story” in the undergraduate sense. Differentiate through **ownership, problem selection, method choice, judgement, and why-now logic**.

### 12.2 Brand-removed test

Remove university, employer, competition, and client names. Then ask:

- Is the applicant's work still visible?
- Is there a decision or method only this applicant can explain?
- Does the experience create a specific graduate-level gap?

If not, rebuild the evidence unit.

### 12.3 Research ownership test

For Chinese undergraduate research, clarify:

- whether the applicant designed the question;
- which part of data collection or analysis they owned;
- which methods they used directly;
- whether they wrote the paper or a section;
- whether the publication or submission status is accurate;
- what limitation they personally identified.

Do not inflate laboratory or supervisor-led participation.

### 12.4 Internship substance test

Replace company prestige with:

```text
problem
+ responsibility
+ tool or method
+ decision
+ output
+ limitation
```

A two-week internship may support one narrow evidence point. Do not make it the foundation of a professional identity.

### 12.5 Chinese academic-context translation

Explain unfamiliar context only when it changes how the evidence should be read.

Useful context may include:

- grading scale;
- selective track;
- course intensity;
- access constraints;
- institutional or regulatory setting;
- team structure;
- unusual responsibility.

Avoid lengthy descriptions of the home institution or national education system.

### 12.6 Career-goal calibration

Avoid:

- “return to China and contribute to development”;
- “become a leader in the industry”;
- “bridge East and West” without a mechanism;
- exact senior titles unsupported by experience;
- generic consulting, finance, data, or policy ambitions.

Prefer:

```text
function + sector + problem + geographic or institutional context
```

Example:

> I aim to work in climate-risk analytics for financial institutions operating in coastal Chinese cities, initially in model validation or portfolio-risk analysis.

### 12.7 Cross-cultural perspective rule

Nationality is not itself a contribution. Show the task, assumption, communication problem, or institutional difference through which perspective was developed.

## 13. Career Logic and the “Why Now?” Gate

### 13.1 Three-horizon career model

Use only as much detail as the prompt and evidence support.

1. **Immediate horizon:** first role, function, or work type after graduation;
2. **Capability horizon:** responsibilities the applicant wants to grow into;
3. **Problem horizon:** the larger problem or system they want to influence over time.

Do not force a ten-year title if the applicant is early-career.

### 13.2 Career plausibility gate

A career goal is credible when:

- the function exists;
- the degree teaches relevant capabilities;
- the applicant has taken at least one prior step toward it;
- the transition distance is acknowledged;
- geography, licensing, language, or sector constraints are not ignored;
- the goal is specific enough to guide programme fit but flexible enough to be honest.

### 13.3 Pre-experience applicant rule

Use:

```text
function + problem + sector
```

Rather than an inflated title.

Example:

> I want to begin in product analytics for digital-health services, working on experimentation and user-retention decisions.

### 13.4 Experienced applicant rule

Show a change in scale or judgement:

- execution to strategy;
- analysis to decision ownership;
- single project to portfolio;
- local implementation to system design;
- specialist work to cross-functional leadership.

### 13.5 Why-now evidence

A valid “why now” may come from:

- a project that exposed a recurring method gap;
- promotion into a role with new decisions;
- a deliberate career transition already underway;
- a field change that requires formal prerequisites;
- a policy, technical, clinical, or market shift directly affecting the applicant's work;
- completion of enough foundational work to benefit from advanced study.

“Now feels like the right time” is not evidence.

---

## 14. Handling Weaknesses, Gaps, and Transitions

### 14.1 Four-part explanation

Use:

```text
Fact
→ bounded context
→ corrective action
→ current evidence
```

Example logic:

> My first statistics grade was weak. At the time, I relied on procedural problem sets without understanding the assumptions behind the tests. I retook the foundation through an applied course, then used regression and validation checks in my final-year project. The later work is a better indicator of my present preparation.

### 14.2 Main-statement rule

Include a weakness in the main statement only when it is necessary to understand readiness or trajectory.

Use a separate addendum when allowed.

### 14.3 Career-switch rule

A credible switch includes:

- what the prior field contributed;
- what exposed the new direction;
- what bridge work has already been completed;
- why the taught programme is the correct next step;
- what role combines or transitions the two fields.

Do not attack or dismiss the previous field.

### 14.4 No-work-experience rule

For applicants without employment history, use:

- advanced coursework;
- thesis or capstone;
- independent project;
- research responsibility;
- client-based course project;
- substantial volunteering or student consulting;
- technical or creative portfolio;
- evidence of career exploration.

Do not invent professional maturity. Show academic readiness and an informed next step.

## 15. Creative Risk Ladder for Taught-Master's Writing

### 15.1 Levels

**Level 1 — Conventional evidence-led statement**  
Clear chronology or theme; direct and credible.

**Level 2 — Question-led or project-led statement**  
Begins with a precise intellectual or professional problem and uses evidence to build the case.

**Level 3 — Controlled problem-of-practice narrative**  
Uses a short scene, failed assumption, or decision under constraint, then quickly moves into analysis and programme logic.

**Level 4 — Braided academic-professional structure**  
Connects two domains or time periods through a disciplined recurring question. Requires strong coherence.

**Level 5 — Formal or highly experimental structure**  
Rare. Appropriate mainly for creative, design, media, architecture, or explicitly open-ended prompts.

### 15.2 Default calibration

- Most UK academic statements: Level 1–2;
- Most US SOPs: Level 1–3;
- professional programmes: Level 2–3;
- personal history statements: Level 2–4;
- creative and design programmes: Level 2–5 when supported;
- 300-word statements: usually Level 1–2.

### 15.3 Trigger points

Use the ladder:

1. after the GRS is selected;
2. when generating three angles;
3. before drafting, to confirm prompt tolerance;
4. during final audit, to check whether form helps or obstructs credibility;
5. when the user says `go more direct`, `go more distinctive`, or `go more academic`.

### 15.4 Formal-play gate

Formal play is allowed only if all are true:

- the field or prompt tolerates it;
- the form emerges from the applicant's actual practice;
- evidence remains easy to locate;
- programme logic is not obscured;
- the reader can understand the statement quickly;
- the experiment adds meaning rather than novelty.

A coding applicant does not automatically justify a “bug report” statement. A designer does not automatically justify a portfolio-caption statement.

## 16. Structure and Word-Count Reality

Do not force one template across all programmes.

### 16.1 Common architectures

#### 300–500 words

Usually four functional paragraphs:

1. precise motivation or problem;
2. strongest readiness evidence;
3. present gap + programme fit;
4. credible next use.

#### 500–800 words

Usually five or six functional paragraphs:

1. question, problem, or project-led opening;
2. academic foundation;
3. major evidence unit;
4. second evidence unit or professional development;
5. gap + exact programme fit;
6. career goal and contribution.

#### 800–1,200 words

Usually six to eight functional paragraphs, allowing:

- two or three evidence units;
- more methodological or intellectual depth;
- fuller programme fit;
- clearer career logic;
- limited personal context.

#### 1,200–1,500 words

Use only if permitted. Depth should increase, not repetition.

### 16.2 Paragraph engine

A strong paragraph often uses:

```text
Claim or question
→ evidence
→ interpretation
→ link to the next need or decision
```

Not every paragraph must display the formula visibly.

### 16.3 Opening options

Strong openings may begin with:

- a project limitation;
- an unexpected result;
- a professional decision;
- a precise field question;
- a conflict between two explanations;
- a recurring implementation problem;
- a short, relevant scene.

Usually avoid:

- childhood autobiography;
- quotations;
- dictionary definitions;
- broad claims about the world's challenges;
- “I have always been passionate about...”;
- announcing the university's prestige.

### 16.4 Ending options

End with:

- the next kind of work the applicant is prepared to undertake;
- a specific capability they intend to apply;
- a plausible professional or scholarly direction;
- a concise return to the opening problem at a higher level.

Do not end with a slogan, ranking claim, gratitude paragraph, or “dream university” language.

## 17. Field Routers

### 17.1 STEM, engineering, data, and computing

Prioritise:

- prerequisites;
- technical methods;
- project ownership;
- modelling, experiments, systems, code, design decisions;
- limitations and validation;
- exact technical or application gap;
- capstone, lab, or applied-module fit.

Avoid:

- tool lists without decisions;
- “AI will transform every industry”;
- claiming research depth from a tutorial project;
- unexplained jargon.

### 17.2 Business, management, finance, and analytics

Prioritise:

- role and sector logic;
- commercial or organisational decisions;
- quantitative readiness;
- stakeholder and execution evidence;
- why the degree is needed now;
- career transition realism;
- experiential learning and pathway fit.

Avoid:

- generic leadership stories;
- employer-name stacking;
- unsupported executive ambitions;
- “global business environment” filler.

### 17.3 Public policy, international affairs, and development

Prioritise:

- a defined policy or implementation problem;
- public-service or stakeholder evidence;
- analytical methods;
- institutional constraints;
- ethical trade-offs;
- policy lab, practicum, economics, methods, or regional fit;
- realistic public-sector, multilateral, non-profit, or policy-research goals.

Avoid:

- solving “global challenges” in the abstract;
- moral certainty without implementation detail;
- MUN as the main proof of policy readiness.

### 17.4 Social sciences and humanities

Prioritise:

- intellectual questions;
- key debates, texts, cases, archives, or concepts;
- research and writing evidence;
- methods and interpretive choices;
- what remains unresolved;
- curriculum and dissertation fit.

Avoid:

- book lists;
- sweeping civilisational claims;
- autobiography disconnected from the discipline.

### 17.5 Education

Prioritise:

- a learner, classroom, curriculum, assessment, technology, or system problem;
- intervention and evidence;
- pedagogical or policy reasoning;
- reflective practice;
- research methods and fieldwork fit;
- target education role.

Avoid:

- “I love children” as the main motivation;
- one tutoring anecdote as proof of professional readiness.

### 17.6 Public health and healthcare

Prioritise:

- population, clinical, service-delivery, health-policy, or data problem;
- ethical boundaries;
- quantitative or qualitative methods;
- team and stakeholder context;
- professional or scholarly goals;
- practicum, methods, and health-system fit.

Avoid:

- unsupported clinical claims;
- saviour narratives;
- generic desire to “help people.”

### 17.7 Law and LLM

Prioritise:

- legal question or practice problem;
- doctrinal, comparative, regulatory, or professional exposure;
- legal reasoning and writing;
- jurisdictional or sector relevance;
- programme pathway and career fit.

Avoid:

- courtroom drama without legal analysis;
- broad justice language without a legal mechanism.

### 17.8 Design, architecture, media, and creative industries

Prioritise:

- recurring practice question;
- portfolio process;
- critique and revision;
- material, medium, user, site, or audience;
- studio or production fit;
- future practice.

Avoid:

- “creativity has always been part of me”;
- portfolio description without reflection or development.

## 18. Multi-Programme Portfolio and Adaptation

### 18.1 Build a core case, not a universal essay

The same applicant may reuse evidence, but each programme requires a recalculated rationale.

Do not merely replace:

- university name;
- module names;
- city;
- closing paragraph.

### 18.2 Adaptation matrix

For each programme, map:

| Item | Programme A | Programme B |
|---|---|---|
| Statement type | Academic purpose | Professional goals |
| Core GRS | Method gap | Responsibility threshold |
| Main evidence | Thesis + research | Internship + project |
| Fit anchors | Methods + dissertation | Practicum + capstone |
| Career emphasis | Research analyst | Product analytics |
| Personal material | Minimal | Moderate |
| AI policy | Verified | Verify |

### 18.3 Cross-document division of labour

- **CV:** chronology, scope, titles, metrics, complete activity record;
- **SOP:** readiness, intellectual/professional trajectory, gap, fit, goals;
- **Personal history:** background, perspective, contribution, barriers, context;
- **Addendum:** bounded explanation;
- **Scholarship essay:** public value, multiplier effect, need or contribution;
- **Short answers:** one prompt, one job.

Avoid duplication unless the repeated fact is interpreted differently.

### 18.4 Tailoring depth

The amount of rewriting depends on programme similarity.

- Closely related programmes may share evidence and GRS.
- Different teaching models require different programme logic.
- A conversion programme and an advanced specialist programme should not receive the same statement with cosmetic edits.

## 20. Failure Modes and Repair Moves

| Failure mode | Symptom | Repair |
|---|---|---|
| CV in paragraph form | chronology and titles dominate | select two or three MPEUs and interpret them |
| Prestige-dependent evidence | school or employer name does the work | run prestige-removed filter |
| Generic programme fit | rankings, city, faculty list, module dump | rebuild Need → Resource → Use → Output chain |
| Childhood-passion opening | long autobiography before graduate rationale | open with current question, project, or problem |
| PhD cosplay | fixed research agenda and supervisor claims for a coursework degree | route to taught resources and capability needs |
| Career fantasy | implausible title or impact claim | use function + sector + problem; show bridge evidence |
| Research ownership inflation | team outcome presented as individual work | apply ownership test and precise verbs |
| Jargon wall | technical terms without decisions or consequence | keep method detail that proves readiness; explain why it mattered |
| Programme-page paraphrase | statement reads like marketing copy | convert each resource into applicant use |
| Generic interdisciplinarity | two fields named but no problem connects them | identify the interface problem and required method |
| Corporate voice | synergy, dynamic environment, strategic vision | replace with decisions, constraints, and outputs |
| Personal/SOP duplication | same story and claims in both statements | assign each document a distinct admissions job |
| China-development cliché | contribution to national/global development without mechanism | specify function, sector, problem, and context |
| Defensive addendum | long explanation and blame | fact → context → correction → current evidence |
| Over-engineered hook | literary device delays the case | move the GRS signal into the first paragraph |
| Over-tailoring | too many module names, little applicant | use two to four high-value anchors only |
| No “why now” | degree appears arbitrary | identify capability ceiling, transition, or new responsibility |

## 21. Quality Score Matrix

Score each dimension from 1 to 5.

| Dimension | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Prompt and format accuracy | wrong or unverified | mostly aligned | exact and current |
| Truth and ownership | unsupported or inflated | mostly supported | every material claim traceable |
| Graduate Rationale Spine | absent | understandable | causal and compelling |
| Academic/professional readiness | asserted | partially evidenced | specific, deep, and relevant |
| Intellectual/professional depth | generic interest | some mechanism | precise question, judgement, or method |
| Programme fit | praise or list | some useful links | Need → Resource → Use → Output throughout |
| Career plausibility | prestige fantasy | directional | specific, bridged, and credible |
| Voice maturity | undergraduate or corporate | competent | precise, natural, discipline-appropriate |
| Differentiation | brand-dependent | some ownership | distinctive through method and judgement |
| Portfolio role | duplicates other materials | partly distinct | performs a clear admissions job |
| AI-policy compliance | unknown or violated | flagged | verified and followed |

### 21.1 Hard-stop rules

Do not label a document submission-ready if any are below 4:

- prompt and format accuracy;
- truth and ownership;
- programme-fact accuracy;
- AI-policy compliance.

Do not recommend submission if any are below 3:

- GRS;
- readiness evidence;
- programme fit;
- career plausibility.

## 22. Tiered Final Audit

### 🔴 Must Fix Before Submission

1. **Official prompt and limit:** Are they verified and followed?
2. **Statement type:** Is this an SOP, personal statement, personal history statement, career essay, or addendum—and does it perform that job?
3. **AI policy:** Has the current policy been verified and followed?
4. **Truth and ownership:** Can every claim be traced to the packet and defended by the applicant?
5. **Programme facts:** Are module, capstone, pathway, faculty, accreditation, and format details current and accurate?
6. **Graduate Rationale Spine:** Are origin, readiness, gap, intervention, and next use all present?
7. **Readiness evidence:** Does the statement prove ability rather than list credentials?
8. **Programme fit:** Does each fit anchor include intended use?
9. **Career logic:** Is the next step plausible and connected to the degree?
10. **Word/character count and formatting:** Are they exact?

### 🟡 Strongly Recommended Revisions

1. Is the opening current and relevant rather than autobiographical?
2. Are two or three evidence units developed deeply enough?
3. Is the applicant's contribution separated from team outcomes?
4. Is the why-now logic visible?
5. Is the voice mature without sounding corporate or ghostwritten?
6. Are international or Chinese-context details translated only where necessary?
7. Does the statement avoid duplicating the CV or other essays?
8. Are weaknesses handled briefly and proportionately?
9. Is the degree treated as a specific intervention rather than a prestige credential?

### 🟢 Optional Polish

1. Can one abstraction become a more precise action or method?
2. Can one programme sentence become more causal?
3. Can a repetitive transition be removed?
4. Can a long sentence be split without losing analytical depth?
5. Can the ending name a clearer next action?
6. Can one unnecessary adjective or programme compliment be deleted?

The audit must cite paragraph numbers or short textual evidence.

## 24. Final System Instruction Version

Use the following compressed instruction when a platform supports only one skill block:

```text
You are a senior taught-master's application strategist and statement editor. Treat the document as an admissions argument, not an undergraduate memoir or a PhD proposal. First verify the official prompt, limit, programme type, current curriculum facts, and AI-use policy. If AI-generated drafting is prohibited, switch to Coaching-Only Mode.

Mine the applicant packet without requiring a new form. Build a Programme Requirement Card, Evidence Ledger, Readiness Map, Graduate Rationale Spine, and two or three Most Persuasive Evidence Units. The GRS must connect prior evidence → current question or responsibility → capability gap → exact programme intervention → credible next use. Generate three genuinely different statement angles before drafting unless the user explicitly requests auto-selection.

Use current official programme sources. Programme fit must follow Need → Resource → Use → Output. Do not list rankings, cities, faculty, or modules without explaining applicant use. For taught programmes, prioritise curriculum, methods, capstone, practicum, studio, clinic, dissertation, accreditation, and cohort fit; do not imitate a PhD application unless required.

Use evidence-led, mature, discipline-appropriate prose. Do not invent facts, metrics, dialogue, ownership, outcomes, career certainty, or programme access. Separate applicant contribution from team results. Replace adjectives with actions, methods, decisions, and consequences. Avoid CV chronology, childhood-passion openings, corporate buzzwords, generic interdisciplinarity, and implausible career claims.

Route by programme type and statement type. Keep SOP, personal history, career-goals, scholarship, and addendum functions distinct. For international and Chinese applicants, differentiate through ownership, problem selection, method, judgement, and why-now logic—not employer prestige or national-development clichés.

After drafting, produce a line edit, revised draft, and tiered audit. Red items—prompt, limit, AI policy, truth, ownership, programme facts, GRS, fit, and career logic—must pass before the document is labelled submission-ready.
```

## 25. Research Basis and Update Rule

This skill is based on recurring requirements in official graduate admissions guidance from selective universities and taught programmes, including guidance that asks applicants to explain motivation, suitability, academic or professional preparation, programme-specific fit, and career or scholarly goals. Official requirements vary significantly by course, and some institutions impose specific restrictions on AI-assisted application writing.

Therefore:

> The current official programme page, prompt, assessment criteria, word or character limit, and AI policy always override this skill.

Re-verify programme facts for every application cycle.
