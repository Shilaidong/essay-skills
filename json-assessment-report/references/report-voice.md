# Report Voice Reference

## Voice

Write like a careful counselor explaining evidence to a student:

- Warm, direct, concrete.
- Professional without sounding clinical.
- Simple sentences.
- No mechanical "AI analysis" language.
- No change logs or internal notes inside the report.

Prefer:

- "You may feel pressure when scores become a comparison."
- "This does not mean you lack ability. It means this situation touches self-worth."
- "This part has too little information, so it should stay as a note."

Avoid:

- "Based on multidimensional psychometric modeling..."
- "The AI concludes..."
- "The report was modified to..."
- "This proves you are..."
- "You are a high-neuroticism person."

## Student-safe Interpretation

For sensitive labels:

- Translate "neuroticism" as "emotional sensitivity" or similar if the audience is a student.
- Avoid diagnosis words unless the source is clinical and the user specifically asks for clinical language.
- Use strengths and pressure triggers together.

Pattern:

```text
What the data shows -> What it may feel like -> What to do next
```

Example:

```text
Your agreeableness score is high, and you described pulling a friend back into a group discussion. This suggests you notice when someone might be left out. That is a strength. The caution is that you do not need to carry everyone's feelings every time.
```

## Evidence Writing

Each evidence card should have:

- A short finding title.
- One short quote or data point.
- A plain-language reading.

Keep quotes short. If a raw quote is long, extract the strongest clause and paraphrase the rest.

## Action Advice

Actions should be small enough to do:

- "Make one pull-quote card per week."
- "Compare this week's score only with last week's score."
- "Create a folder with score evidence, project evidence, and activity evidence."

Avoid vague advice:

- "Improve yourself."
- "Be more confident."
- "Explore more."

Turn vague advice into observable next steps.
