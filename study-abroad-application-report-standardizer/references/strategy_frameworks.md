# Strategy Frameworks

## Applicant positioning dimensions

Most students can be described through a mix of:

- Academic spike.
- Research spike.
- Impact/leadership spike.
- Creative/portfolio spike.
- Professional/career spike.
- Entrepreneurial spike.
- Community/personal-context spike.
- Writing/humanities spike.

Avoid generic labels unless they drive decisions.

## Evidence chain

A strong plan links:

```text
student origin -> question/problem -> academic preparation -> sustained work -> measurable evidence -> external validation -> school/program fit -> application narrative
```

If any link is missing, design the next action around that gap.

## Route architecture

### Single-track

Use when one academic/professional direction dominates.

Plan:

- Build depth.
- Avoid unrelated expansion.
- Use all essays and recommendations to reinforce one identity.

### Dual-track

Use when two directions are plausible but not both final.

Plan:

- Define shared evidence core.
- Build distinct evidence for each track.
- Set a decision gate.
- Stop the weaker track before final applications if it dilutes coherence.

### Multi-country

Use when the same student applies to different systems.

Plan:

- U.K./Europe-style systems often need direct academic fit.
- U.S.-style systems often allow broader exploration.
- Canada/Australia/Hong Kong/Singapore may be program-specific but vary by institution.
- Do not copy the same essay logic across systems without adaptation.

### Portfolio route

Use for architecture, design, art, music, theatre, film, or similar.

Evidence:

- Process, not only final product.
- Original work.
- Iteration and critique.
- Technical skill.
- Conceptual maturity.
- Portfolio requirements per school.

### Graduate/professional route

Evidence:

- Prerequisite coursework.
- Research/professional experience.
- Faculty or lab fit.
- SOP logic.
- Recommendations from academic/professional supervisors.
- CV and writing sample.

## Activity design standards

Each flagship activity should pass the 10-question test:

1. Why is this student's story connected to the problem?
2. What is the exact question or pain point?
3. Who benefits?
4. What method will be used?
5. What will be produced in 4, 8, and 12 weeks?
6. What numbers will be tracked?
7. What qualitative evidence will be collected?
8. Who can verify the work?
9. What can go wrong?
10. How will this appear in essays and recommendations?

## Risk categories

- Academic risk.
- Testing risk.
- Direction/coherence risk.
- Evidence/verification risk.
- Timeline risk.
- Financial risk.
- Application-round risk.
- Recommender risk.
- Ethics/privacy risk.
- Document-quality risk.

## Decision gates

A decision gate must include:

- Date.
- Required evidence.
- Minimum quality threshold.
- What happens if the threshold is met.
- What happens if it is not met.

Example:

```text
By 2027-03-31, choose Design Track only if the student has two critiqued portfolio projects, one physical or digital prototype, and a recommender who can discuss design process. Otherwise pivot to urban studies/public policy route.
```
